# Cleanway — Brutally Honest Competitive Audit

*An internal DD-style read of where we actually stand on quality, recall, speed, and real-world help — as of 2026-06-29.*

---

## TL;DR

Cleanway is a sharp, single-purpose anti-phishing tool with a defensible privacy posture and a credibility moat (publicly reproducible head-to-head benchmark, planned MIT engine split, domain-only contract enforced at the schema layer). It is also a product with **zero monthly active users**, a still-unrouted DNS hostname, a public endpoint that until very recently returned `unknown` on 50/50 fresh phishing URLs in our own published benchmark, a 5-checks/minute rate limiter that rejects cache hits, and a Granny Mode with no onboarding path so the elderly users it was designed for cannot reach it without paying for a Family plan first. The engineering is real. The strategy is half-shipped. The window to stop polishing credibility and start shipping to actual users is closing.

**Top 5 real strengths**

1. **Mechanical privacy enforcement** — `DomainStr` schema cap at 253 chars rejects full URLs at the API edge; LLM judge enforces a `SAFE_KEYS` whitelist; Sentry scrubber + audit log IP hashing layered on top. This is code, not policy.
2. **Reproducible head-to-head benchmark vs Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 for Families** with a `scripts/eval_fresh_urls.py` clone-and-run script and weekly GH Actions cron. No incumbent publishes this.
3. **Surface breadth at solo velocity** — Chrome MV3 + Firefox + Safari + Outlook taskpane + RN mobile + Next.js landing + DoH gateway + iOS `.mobileconfig`. (Caveat: breadth without users is debt, not an asset.)
4. **Hot-cache latency** — 24h Redis cache with cross-request singleflight coalescing; server-side `x-response-time: 1.6ms` for cached domains. Cold path 19-source fan-out completes in published p50 ~287ms (CI-measured, not prod-observed).
5. **Cultural & persona-aware UX layer** — 10-locale block page, Granny voice alerts, 50 hand-curated locale × category explainer payloads, Honeypot Shield, conformal `confidence_pct` chips. No competitor ships this combination.

**Top 5 real weaknesses**

1. **Zero MAU. Zero installs verified in any store. Zero external benchmark validation.** Every "moat" in this document is theoretical until someone other than the author runs the product.
2. **The publicly committed benchmark file (`docs/benchmarks/2026-06-17-fresh-urls.md`) currently shows Cleanway 0% vs Cloudflare 60%** because it was generated against the rule-only public endpoint before the fan-out upgrade. The 93.5% counter-number lives only in a memory file we wrote about ourselves and has not been re-run.
3. **Production bug: the 5-fresh-checks/minute IP rate limiter increments before the cache check** (`api/routers/public.py:115-133` before `:145`), so users get HTTP 429 on warm domains. Reproduced live in five curl calls.
4. **Granny Mode has no activation funnel.** It only fires via `options.html` or Family-plan enrollment ($5/mo). The solo elderly user it was built for cannot turn it on. Strategy #15's cultural explainer endpoint is also shipped but has zero callsites in the extension — grep confirms one comment, no fetch.
5. **`dns.cleanway.ai` CNAME is still unset** (`docs/PLAYBOOK_TONIGHT.md` item #1). The iOS `.mobileconfig` profiles install but resolve `NXDOMAIN`. The "system-wide phishing block without an app" pitch is currently false in production.

## Dimension 1 — Cleanway vs paid consumer security suites

Norton 360 Deluxe, McAfee Total Protection, and Bitdefender Total Security each bundle VPN, ID-theft monitoring, password manager, parental controls, cloud backup, firewall, and a full AV engine for $50–100/yr. They each carry 20+ years of AV-Comparatives anti-phishing certification (≥85% block rate on a 275-URL externally curated test, re-certified quarterly). Norton ships with ~50M consumer users; Bitdefender claims ~500M endpoints; McAfee is pre-installed on millions of new PCs.

Cleanway has none of that. We do not ship AV, VPN, password manager, parental controls, or any binary/process-level defense. We have not been submitted to AV-Comparatives. Our **AUC of 0.9983 on 14.4K samples is not comparable** to a 275-URL externally curated phishing-block-rate test — those numbers live on different axes, and the analyst originally treated them as if they did not. Bitdefender's TrafficLight trains on hundreds of millions of URLs/day in real telemetry; our training corpus is 7,200 phish + 7,200 legit, suspiciously balanced for a domain where the real-world base rate is well under 1%, which is the kind of number that screams "AUC overfit to a small balanced set" and tells us almost nothing about false-positive behavior at scale.

What we genuinely have is (a) a **domain-only privacy contract** that the suites' business model cannot copy without rebuilding their telemetry economics — though "architecturally cannot copy" is the wrong word; Bitdefender *could* ship a privacy-only SKU, they *don't* because it would cannibalize the suite; (b) **published per-domain head-to-head benchmarks** with a clone-and-run script, which is genuinely novel for the category; (c) **DoH + `.mobileconfig` reach** that doesn't require a heavy installer with kernel/root permissions. The "Granny/Kids gap" the analyst initially claimed is wrong — Norton Family and Bitdefender Parental Control exist; we serve those personas *differently*, not *uniquely*, and there is no UX study to back even the "differently better" claim.

Honest read: do not pitch Cleanway as a Norton replacement. The average $60/yr suite buyer is not shopping on privacy. The pitch is "the privacy-respecting phishing layer that lives alongside or under the OS, router, carrier, or Outlook/Gmail tenant." That positioning is defensible. Trying to play the suite game is not.

**Direction score: 7** — right segment for the right reason, but contingent on NOT competing as a suite and on actually closing a layer/bundle deal (none currently exist, signed or in pilot).

## Dimension 2 — Cleanway vs free defaults (Google Safe Browsing, Cloudflare 1.1.1.1 for Families, SmartScreen)

The free defaults are pre-installed in ~3B Chrome installs, Edge, Safari, and every Windows machine. They cost nothing, require no consent, have no install friction, and update via background hash-prefix pushes (GSB) every ~30 minutes. Cleanway is none of those things.

What Cleanway *can* defensibly claim against the free defaults:

- **Fresh-URL recall** — but only on the basis of the post-fan-out 93.5% figure that has not been independently re-benchmarked. The publicly committed benchmark currently shows the *opposite* (Cleanway 0%, Cloudflare 60%) and that is the artifact a tough reviewer will read first.
- **Cultural, persona-tuned explanations** — 10 locales × 5 categories of hand-curated cultural payloads (Сбер/Госуслуги/Sparkasse/PIX/UPI). Real, but 5 cells per locale and **no evidence of native-speaker QA** for Hindi, Arabic, or Indonesian (which are flagged "usable not good" in our own memory).
- **Active protection** — Honeypot Shield (Strategy #8). Real code, **zero validation against actual phishing kits**. Most modern credential-harvesting kits use keystroke logging or client-side JS that bypasses native setters, and we have no metric for "% of real kits that accepted the fake watermark."

The "stronger privacy posture" claim needs an asterisk we initially elided: the LLM judge ships outbound to Anthropic on every caution-band verdict. The boundary is "no full URLs, no page content" — not "zero third-party egress." GSB sends hashed URL prefixes to Google. Trust boundary, not absence.

The brutal truth the original analyst hand-waved: free defaults are installed *before* the phishing attempt; Cleanway is installed *after* the user already cares. The addressable market for a "better than free" anti-phishing tool is "people who have already lost money once," which is a brutal acquisition channel.

**Direction score: 6** — right axis, but headline detection-rate claim is not currently reflected in the published artifact, the cultural-explainer pitch is unwired in the extension UI (see Dimension 5), and Outlook AppSource integration has effectively zero realistic install funnel without enterprise sales motion that does not exist.

## Dimension 3 — DNS-level filtering vs NextDNS, DNSFilter, ControlD, CleanBrowsing

This is the weakest lane in the Cleanway story. NextDNS has ~100 filter categories, per-device profiles, anycast PoPs, IPv4/v6 unicast, DoT in addition to DoH, native apps, router/AppleTV/console guides, and years of uptime. DNSFilter has SOC 2, MSP multi-tenancy, AI category engine, ~30s threat-feed propagation. ControlD has geo-rewrite and split-horizon. CleanBrowsing has free school deployments at scale.

Cleanway has: one DoH endpoint behind a single Railway region (sin1), no dashboard, no profiles, no categories, no native app, no router config, no IPv4/v6 unicast, no DoT, no anycast, and **`dns.cleanway.ai` is not yet routed** so the iOS `.mobileconfig` pitch is currently dead-on-arrival.

What we have that they do not:
- A reproducible head-to-head benchmark methodology — but the methodology page measures the *analyzer*'s recall on the public `/check` endpoint, **not the DoH gateway's recall**. The DoH gateway only blocks domains already warmed into the `dangerous_domains` Redis set, which is a strict subset of what the analyzer catches live. Citing the methodology page as DNS-lane credibility is sleight of hand.
- The same threat-intel set the extension uses — Tranco/favicon/Watchtower/LLM-judge promotion into `dangerous_domains` — which gives us in-page UX surface a pure DNS resolver structurally cannot reach. But this argues for de-positioning DNS as "one surface among many," not as a competitor to NextDNS.

Other gaps the original analyst missed: Android 9+ Private DNS uses **DoT**, not DoH, so the Android pitch on the landing page is functionally broken even with the CNAME live. The iOS `.mobileconfig` signing status is silent — unsigned profiles show "Unverified" on install, which destroys grandma-tier UX. ANTHROPIC_API_KEY is still pending in Railway, so Strategy #21's contribution to `dangerous_domains` is partially aspirational in prod.

**Direction score: 4** — DoH as one surface is conceptually right; as a head-to-head DNS competitor this is sub-MVP. Without the CNAME and an honest re-scoping to "phishing-tuned DoH endpoint, one surface in a multi-surface defense," this lane should not be marketed at all.

## Dimension 4 — Speed

The architectural direction is correct: 24h Redis hot cache with cross-request singleflight coalescing, fail-open circuit breakers per upstream, per-source 3–5s httpx timeouts, local-paint extension UX, 19-source parallel fan-out via `asyncio.gather`. Published Q2 latency: p50=287ms, p95=612ms, p99=1124ms. Live probes from Singapore hit 277–458ms wall-clock with `x-response-time: 1.6ms`.

That paragraph is the marketing version. The honest version has six asterisks:

1. **Singapore-to-Singapore probes are a self-own dressed as evidence.** EU and US users pay 150–300ms of RTT on top, so the headline p50 is irrelevant to the named target market.
2. **The transparency JSON is hand-computed on a CI runner**, not live-observed in prod. `docs/runbooks/infra-readiness.md:165` explicitly notes "No p95 latency dashboard." If we can't measure prod latency we don't know our prod latency.
3. **The 5-checks/minute rate limiter increments before the cache check.** Reproduced live: five sequential curls on `google.com`/`youtube.com`/`wikipedia.org`/`github.com` (all warm) all returned 429 from the same IP. The 24h cache investment is being undermined by a bug fixable in ten lines.
4. **`scoreLocally()` local-paint is a heuristic, not a blocklist hit.** It can paint GREEN on a domain the API later flags RED. The false-green window during a 1–3s cold call is unquantified and arguably worse than a spinner.
5. **Singleflight is an in-process Python dict (`_INFLIGHT`)**, not Redis-backed. On multi-worker Railway it does NOT coalesce across workers, so the "10,000 users → 1 fan-out" claim is wrong at any non-trivial scale.
6. **Fail-open circuit breakers are a recall hit**, not just a speed win. When PhishTank is degraded we degrade the verdict, not just the latency.

The Bloom-filter / client-side hash-table architecture promised in `STRATEGY-2026-06-16.md:413` that would close the structural gap vs Google Safe Browsing **lives as an empty `bloom/` directory**. GSB ships hash prefixes to ~3B clients every 30 minutes; we ship `cache-control: no-store` from a single Singapore region with no CDN.

**Direction score: 5** (revised down from initial 6) — bones are right, but unbuilt bloom + live-reproducible 429 + single region + no CDN + no prod observability is not a 6 on a tough panel.

## Dimension 5 — Real-world UX for non-technical users (the grandma test)

This dimension is where Cleanway's intent is most genuinely differentiated and where the execution gap is most embarrassing.

What we genuinely shipped: skill-level personas (Kids/Regular/Granny/Pro), font upscaling, localized Web Speech voice alerts in 10 locales, a 4-card annotated evidence pattern, a `confidence_pct` chip, the "Keep my password safe" honeypot button (consciously revised from "Send fake password" on grandma-ethics grounds), and progressive disclosure in the popup (3 visible elements + a collapsed `<details>`).

What we did not ship — and what makes the "real-world UX" framing currently aspirational:

- **No onboarding picker.** A grandma installing Cleanway is dropped into "regular" mode by default and has no path to discover Granny Mode exists. The only activation routes are (a) `chrome-extension://.../options.html` which she will never open, or (b) Family Hub enrollment by a tech-savvy relative, gated behind a $5/mo plan. **The most-vulnerable users — solo elderly without tech-savvy family — cannot get the feature designed for them at all.** This is a damning unit-economics finding.
- **The Strategy #15 cultural explainer is unwired.** `grep -rn 'scam_explainer|/explainer'` across `packages/extension-core/` and `extension/` returns exactly one match — a comment. The backend exists, the LLM cache exists, 20 unit tests pass, and a Russian grandma seeing a Сбер phishing page still gets the *generic* 3-step scheme, not a Сбер-specific narrative. One sales-sheet bullet is currently a lie of omission.
- **Web Speech API has no fallback handling for missing voices.** Russian/Hindi/Indonesian TTS voices are not installed by default on Spanish or English Windows/macOS. A grandma on a Spanish Windows 11 PC hitting a Russian-language scam page gets silent failure.
- **The 5-second countdown + pulsing red + Cyrillic voice yelling "СТОП"** has never been tested for whether it is *helpful* vs *traumatic*. A 70-year-old with cardiac issues seeing this is a real human factor; we are guessing.
- **The "ты-form is appropriate for elderly Russian users" claim** is a sociolinguistic assertion by 30-something engineers with no native-speaker validation. Older Russians often expect вы-form even from family. The "protective grandchild voice" framing could equally land as "machine being rude."
- **"Keep my password safe" is dark-pattern adjacent.** The button still submits a fake watermarked password to the attacker. The user thinks they are being protected; the extension is actively impersonating their submission. Defensible technically; not interrogated honestly in our own copy review.
- **DNS install path completely bypasses all of this.** A grandma who installs the `.mobileconfig` (when the CNAME lands) gets zero personas, zero voice, zero scheme cards — she gets an opaque DNS failure page. Half the "20/20 shipped" surface is irrelevant for those users.

**Direction score: 5** (revised down from initial 6) — right intent, half-built activation funnel, untested with real users, and the DNS surface short-circuits the entire UX layer. Score reflects delivery, not intent.

## Dimension 6 — The "server-blind" privacy claim

This is the strongest engineering story in the audit and also the one whose marketing copy most needs tightening.

The mechanical defense is real and auditable: extension extracts `url.hostname` client-side; API schema is `DomainStr` with a 253-char cap that 422-rejects full URLs by construction; the LLM judge uses a `SAFE_KEYS` whitelist with a sha256(features) cache key that is domain-free; Sentry has a custom scrubber for JWTs/UUIDs/IPs/emails/Stripe IDs; audit log IPs are hashed before write. A DD privacy reviewer can verify all of this in an afternoon.

But "server-blind" as a one-liner overclaims and the original review undersold the leaks:

1. **The public per-domain endpoint puts the domain in the URL path: `GET /api/v1/public/check/{domain}`.** The request-logging middleware's `_scrub_path_for_logs` function only scrubs `/api/v1/breach/check/` and `/api/v1/breach/passwords/`. The `/public/check/{domain}` path is logged verbatim with the queried domain, paired with the client IP, into whatever log sink Railway and Sentry retain. "Verify in DevTools" doesn't show what hits the log sink.
2. **64-bit SHA-256 IP truncation is theater for IPv4.** ~4.3B addresses against a 16-hex-char hash collides ~1:1 — brute-force-recoverable on a laptop in seconds. The "anonymization" only meaningfully protects IPv6.
3. **The fan-out to Safe Browsing, IPQS, AlienVault sends the domain to those third parties.** A user "verifying in DevTools" sees only the first hop and is left with a false sense of completeness.
4. **The LLM judge ships data to Anthropic on every caution-band verdict.** Domain-free, yes — but third-party egress, yes too.
5. **The "server-blind" marketing copy at `landing/app/[locale]/check/[domain]/page.tsx:304` is on a page whose URL itself contains the queried domain.** The tagline is on the literal page that leaks the domain to logs.

The honest tagline is **"domain-only, never URL paths or page content"** — which is still better than Norton/McAfee/Bitdefender's full-URL submission. But uBlock Origin (zero server calls) and DNS-layer filters (1.1.1.1, NextDNS) structurally beat us on privacy purism. Direction is "8 vs the AV category we want to displace, 4 vs the privacy-purist category we cannot displace" — depends which audience.

**Direction score: 8** — engineering is genuinely strong; copy needs tightening, IPv4 hashing needs widening, and the `/public/check/{domain}` log path needs `_scrub_path_for_logs` coverage tonight.

## Dimension 7 — Strategic direction

The thesis (privacy-first + open engine + measured benchmark + multi-surface UX) is coherent. The 93.5% vs 55% Cloudflare delta — *if* it holds on re-benchmark — is genuinely strong PR ammo. The MIT carve-out splitting the engine from billing/weights/intel keys is well-thought-out on paper.

The brutal critique:

- **The "38pp recall delta" is a single 100-URL run with roughly ±10pp binomial confidence interval**, executed by the author against one competitor on one feed (URLhaus/PhishTank — precisely the corpora Cleanway likely trained near, so even "OOD" is suspect without a feed we have never touched). Treating it as a hard number is dishonest.
- **Open-sourcing the engine BEFORE having users is backwards.** HIBP, Tailscale, and Mullvad open-sourced *after* traction. Doing it before launch reads as a solo dev hedging against not getting traction. It also donates the only thing acquirers would value.
- **20/20 strategic items shipped while the core public endpoint had 0% recall** is not "engineering velocity" — it is strategic-item-checklist-completion disguising product-broken-ness. The author was shipping Strategies #15, #17, #21 while the front door was returning `unknown` on every phishing URL.
- **The $500k–3M exit framing is uncritically accepted.** At zero MAU, zero revenue, solo, no enterprise contracts, no SOC 2, no incorporated entity disclosed — the honest acquihire floor is **$0–200k as a code-and-domain sale**, not $500k. A Bitdefender M&A team does not pay $500k for a CatBoost weight file they can re-train in a sprint with their own data.
- **"Brand favicon hashes" as a moat is laughable** — 24 brands hand-seeded, rebuilt in an afternoon by anyone with a phash library.
- **No SOC 2, no insurance, no incorporated entity, GDPR Art. 22 + DSA Art. 14 + Brazil LGPD exposure** across 10 locales for a product that makes automated block decisions with an LLM in the loop. A false negative that costs a user money creates personal liability for a solo operator.

**Direction score: 5** — ideas are right, sequencing is wrong, and the credibility-grooming pattern is postponing the only test that matters: someone other than the author trying the product.
